Executive Summary

The Opportunity

LinkedIn has a monopoly on professional networking — not because it is the best product, but because there is no serious alternative. Vibe is what comes next: a professional network built on personality, short-form video, campus identity, and a personal AI agent that helps every user manage their professional presence across the internet. As of v3.3 the positioning has evolved from "warm professional network" to the network where your AI agent lives.

"Don't make Vibe more professional. Make it unmistakably useful to professionals."

Core thesis
Recruiters do not hate personality. They hate wading through personality to find the signal they need: can this person do the job, are they worth working with, and would they fit here? LinkedIn answers the first question only. Vibe answers all three — and Otto handles the busywork around all three so users can focus on the work itself.
The defensibility argument
A feed, comments, profiles, DMs — LinkedIn could clone Vibe's UI in a quarter. They cannot easily clone Otto. Otto requires per-user voice training (real LLM cost), cross-platform OAuth into competing networks (zero incentive for LinkedIn), and a willingness to ship a character with opinions and autonomy. That last one is a cultural problem LinkedIn cannot solve.
Where Vibe sits
PlatformPositioning
LinkedInFormal + skills-led. The incumbent. Functional but impersonal.
InstagramCasual + personality-led. Great for persona, zero professional utility.
TikTokCasual + content-led. Enormous reach, no professional signal.
HandshakeFunctional + job-led. Solves campus careers, nothing else.
Portfolio sitesFormal + personality. Static, no discovery, no network effect.
VibeWarm professional, with the AI agent that LinkedIn cannot copy.
Where the prototype is
Six fully-built HTML prototype pages plus full Otto mockups. In Next.js, Phase 1 foundation is live: Supabase (schema + RLS), R2, Resend, Sentry, and end-to-end auth routing: signup → email confirm → static Otto onboarding → API persistence → default land on /profile, with server gates on campus/feed/network/messages so users cannot skip Otto or school verification. /profile in the app is still a stub until P1-007. Product Vision

What Vibe Is

Vibe started as "the warm professional network" — a place to show how you think and work, not how your résumé reads. As of v3.3 it's something bigger: a professional operating system with a social network attached. Profile, feed, campus hub, network, opportunities, messages, calendar, and now a personal AI agent named Otto. One verified identity that follows the user from campus to career.

"Your campus, your career, one profile."

Five pillars
  • Profile as portrait — personality-first, with personality and recruiter modes side-by-side. Not a formatted résumé.
  • Vibes — short-form professional video — 60-second pitches with TikTok-style scroll. The elevator-speech you can rewatch.
  • Campus hub — feed, jobs, events, organizations, chat — all in one verified university space. Vibe's answer to the Handshake/GroupMe/Discord fragmentation problem.
  • Otto — the AI marketing agent — every user gets one. Cross-posts to other platforms, watches profile views, drafts replies, surfaces patterns. The defensible moat.
  • Calendar that absorbs everything — RSVPs, interviews, posts, deadlines. Synced across pages. Eventually exportable.
Who Vibe is for
Gen Z and Millennial students and early-career professionals first. Recruiters, campus organizations, and university programs second. The product is built around the candidate experience but designed to be usable — even loved — by the recruiter on the other side of the screen.
The thesis in one sentence
"LinkedIn was built for 40-year-olds with 20 years of experience. Vibe is built for the 14 years before that — with the AI agent that LinkedIn cannot copy."
Design philosophy
Deliberately casual in tone but serious in intent. A hiring manager should find everything they need to make a decision in under a minute. A candidate should feel like they are showing up as a full person, not a formatted résumé. The visual identity does strategic work — the design has to read "serious tool" before the personality content gets the chance to read "human."
Feature Breakdown

Everything Vibe Does

Vibe is six pages plus a calendar plus an AI agent. Six pages share the same left nav (Feed, Network, Campus, Opportunities, Messages) — Profile uses a different layout with a horizontal top nav. Otto sits as a corner ring on every page and gets its own top-level Agent tab. Calendar lives in the left nav of every page and syncs across all of them via localStorage.
Otto is a flagship feature with its own section
The full Otto write-up has its own page in this journal — Otto — Flagship in the left nav. The summary in this Features list is just the short version.
01 — Profile
Personality-first profile, recruiter-ready
A full profile page with a horizontal top nav (different from the rest of the app, intentional). Gradient hero, large avatar, inline-editable everything. Tagline, vibe tags, Currently Into sidebar, posts feed (filterable Top/Recent), Vibes thumbnail grid, work experience cards, sticky section subnav with scroll-spy, skills quick-view row.
Connection stats row shows connections, mutual count (opens a modal listing shared connections), and followers. Edit mode uses contenteditable + a floating B/I/U/Clear toolbar. Avatar and banner support photo upload + gradient picker.
Recruiter Mode
A toggle surfaces a dark Recruiter Snapshot card: role, seniority, location, availability, preferred work type, top skills. Personality sections dim to 35% opacity so professional signal reads first. The same profile, two different views — no separate "résumé page" needed.
Otto on profile (new in v3.3)
A dark Otto status card sits under the hero showing connected platforms and drafts waiting. Per-post badges flag Otto's involvement on each post — orange if syndicated, purple if Otto drafted versions waiting for review, gray for Vibe-only.
02 — Feed
Editorial center column with Vibes strip sidebar
Layout B was selected after weighing options — text posts as the editorial center column, Vibes thumbnails in a right sidebar strip. The feed includes the Voice of the Day (VOTD) prompt at the top, post composer, search, and post cards with reaction system, threaded comments, and per-post Otto badges. Featured posts get a warm coral tint. Hover lifts the card with a subtle coral border.
03 — Vibes
Short-form professional video
TikTok-style full-screen vertical scroll, snap-to-card. Three tabs: For You, Following, Trending. Curated by professional signal (skills overlap, mutuals, field, recency) rather than raw engagement. Each Vibe is up to 60 seconds — long enough for a real elevator pitch, short enough to rewatch.
Reaction system (no likes, no shares, no vanity metrics)
Five reactions, each with its own glow color and burst animation. Fire (excellent), Mind-blown (surprised me), Lightbulb (insightful), Heart hands (appreciate this), Bookmark (saving this). The reaction tells the recruiter what the community values about a candidate, not just whether people clicked.
04 — Comments
Threaded comments with replies
Clicking the comment icon on any post opens a slide-up modal with the original post preview, threaded comments, nested replies (one level deep — keeps it readable), sort by Top or Recent, like individual comments, inline composer. Posting updates the count live without refresh. Reply click pre-fills the @mention. Custom SVG icons throughout — no emoji.
05 — Opportunities
Jobs and internships board
Three-column layout: filter bar and nav on the left, opportunity cards with match percentage in the center, full detail panel on the right. Each listing shows role, type, pay, location, match percent, and the recruiter's profile + a Vibe preview. "Apply with Vibe" CTA replaces generic apply buttons — your application is your Vibe.
Recruiter Mode
Recruiters switch to a posting view, fill out the Post Opportunity modal (role, type, pay, duration, description, required skills), and see applicant Vibes and profiles side-by-side. This is Vibe's core monetisation surface for the recruiter side — paid seats to post and see applicants.
06 — Messages
Direct messaging with a request queue
Three-column layout: thread list with unread badges, message thread view with bubbles and timestamps, search at the top. Non-connected users land in a separate message request queue — preview the message, then Accept or Decline before they enter the inbox. Vibes can be shared inline as preview cards. Typing indicator shows animated dots when the other person is composing.
07 — Network
Connections, Following, Followers in one view
Three sub-tabs covering the full relationship graph. Each card shows avatar, name, role, mutual connection count, and a "why you're seeing this" badge. Pending connection requests expand inline with Accept and Decline buttons. Live search filters instantly across all three tabs.
The relationship model: Follow is one-way for content discovery. Connect is mutual and requires acceptance. Four button states across the app — none, following, pending, connected — with automatic label and style updates. Owner-mode hides the buttons on your own profile.
08 — Campus
A full university platform — not just a tab
Vibe's answer to the Handshake / GroupMe / Discord fragmentation problem. Five complete sections in a single page, all branded for Indiana University with the verified-student badge gating access:
  • Feed — Campus posts with type tags (Announcement, Event, Career, Club callout). RSVP buttons inline.
  • Jobs and Internships — Filter by type, remote, on-campus. Real seeded listings: Goldman Sachs, Stripe, Amazon, Deloitte, Luddy Research, Notion.
  • Events — Date-block layout, six seeded events, RSVP with attendee counts.
  • Organizations — Eight IU orgs with Join and Follow controls: CS Club, Design @ IU, Venture Club, NSBE, Women in Computing, AI Society, Social Media Club, MSA.
  • Chat — Five live channels with pre-seeded conversations: general, cs-majors, design, job-postings, housing-roommates.
09 — Calendar
Integrated event tracking across the whole app
Two surfaces working together. A mini calendar widget in the left nav of every page (except profile, which puts it in the top nav as an icon) — always visible, orange dots flag days with events. Click "My Calendar" to open the full-screen modal — month grid with event pills on day cells, right sidebar for event details, prev/next month nav.
Create events with name, date, time, location, notes, and a 7-color picker. Equal-size day boxes (aspect-ratio:1 + grid-auto-rows:1fr — important detail). Delete events sync across mini and full views. State persists in localStorage under vibe_cal_events_v1. Designed to absorb RSVPs from the Campus page automatically (sync TBD).
10 — Otto (flagship)
Personal AI marketing agent
Otto is built into Vibe and given to every user. Cross-posts content to the user's other platforms (LinkedIn, Instagram, X, TikTok, YouTube Shorts) with platform-adapted tone. Watches profile views. Drafts replies to recruiters. Surfaces patterns. Trained on the user's own posts so the voice stays theirs. Lives as a corner ring on every page and a top-level Agent tab with five sub-tabs (Today, Metrics, Insights, Customize, Platforms).
Strategic role: Vibe's defensible moat. Other social networks can copy a feed or DMs. They cannot copy "an AI agent that knows your voice, manages five platforms, and shows you who's looking at you." For the full design, voice rules, API constraints, monetization scaffold, and build scope, see the Otto — Flagship section in the left nav.
Underneath all of it
Cross-cutting systems
  • Custom cursor — orange dot (instant, zero lerp) plus trailing ring (lerp 0.28). Real Mac cursor hidden via cursor:none on every element.
  • Design tokens — six CSS variables (cream, charcoal, muted, accent, border, shadow) used consistently across all six pages.
  • No-emoji policy — custom SVG icons throughout the UI. Emoji are permitted inside post body content because that's user-generated, but the chrome is clean.
  • Mock data, seeded — every page is fully populated with realistic seeded content. No empty states yet (next session).
  • localStorage persistence — calendar events, journal data, Otto state. Backend is out of scope for the prototype phase.
  • Hosted in Next.js 16.2.4 — six HTML files served from ~/vibe/public/html/ at localhost:3000/html/<page>.html. The HTML files are the product.
● Flagship Feature · Session 21

Otto — Your Marketing Agent

Otto is a personal AI marketing agent built into Vibe. The thesis: a student's life is hard enough — managing five social platforms shouldn't be one of the things they have to do. Otto cross-posts your content with platform-adapted tone, watches who views your profile, drafts replies to recruiters, and surfaces patterns you wouldn't catch on your own. It's framed as a character — quiet, confident, opinionated — not as a feature. This is the biggest strategic shift in Vibe since the warm-professional positioning.

"think of me as your marketing intern. you write the post, i make sure it lands everywhere it needs to — with the right voice for each place."

Strategic positioning shift
Vibe was "the warm professional network." With Otto, Vibe becomes "the network where your AI agent lives." Otto reframes Vibe from social platform to professional operating system, with a social network attached. That's a much bigger story for users, recruiters, and investors. Otto also creates Vibe's defensible moat — LinkedIn can copy a feed, comments, DMs. They cannot easily copy "an AI agent that knows your voice, manages 5 platforms, and learns who's looking at you."
01 — Otto's character
Jarvis lane, not Wall-E lane
Otto leans nonchalant cool — sleek, confident, dry — over cute helper bot. Decided after weighing both: cute reads too soft for recruiters and ages young; Jarvis ages with the user and reinforces "this is a serious tool that just happens to be friendly." Otto has no face, no body — personality lives entirely in motion and copy.
Voice rules
Short — almost always one sentence. Slightly dry. Honest about its own limits ("the X version isn't great, I'd skip it"). Has opinions ("post this one tomorrow morning, not now"). Never uses exclamation points. Never says "Sure!" or "Happy to help!" Lowercase-leaning, but not all lowercase. Speaks in italic Fraunces in the UI to differentiate from system text.
02 — Visual language
Glowing core, orbiting nodes, dual accents
Otto is a small floating element — coral core surrounded by orbiting nodes and breathing rings. Four canonical states: Idle (slow breathing rings + one orbital node), Thinking (two counter-rotating orbits with multiple nodes, fast core pulse), Briefing (vertical sound bars in alternating coral/purple, like an EQ), Alert (bright pulse with a count chip). Primary color is coral (#FF5C35); secondary accent is deep purple (#7C5CFC) for depth on rings, secondary nodes, and certain UI signals.

04 — The Agent tab
Five sub-tabs forming Otto's command center
Today
The default action view. Hero with Otto + briefing quote + 3 KPIs (views, drafts ready, recruiters). Drafts queue (per-platform versions side-by-side with Approve/Edit/Skip). Otto's Inbox (recruiter signals, network observations, missed opportunities — each with an "otto says:" italic interpretation). Activity log. Plus inline previews of Metrics, Insights, Customize, Platforms.
Metrics
Vibe-first, honest about API gaps. KPI row with data-quality notes ("first-party · full detail" vs "est · linkedin not included"). Vibe Recruiter Intel as the hero section — full names, time-on-profile, what they watched, "Reply →" CTAs. The LinkedIn Premium-killer feature, possible because it's first-party data on Vibe. Cross-platform table shows what Otto can actually verify (full data: Vibe/TikTok/YouTube/Instagram-Business · partial: X · delivery only: LinkedIn). Otto annotation under the table explains why the gaps exist — turning the limitation into transparency.
Insights
Otto as strategist, not dashboard. Sections: Otto's Brief (3-paragraph weekly read in italic Fraunces, with action CTAs); What's working / What's not (concrete patterns with multiplier deltas); Best time to post (heatmap with Otto's interpretation); Recruiter signal strength (HOT/WARM/COOL ranking with verdicts); Peer comparison (top X% of CS students at IU). Every section has an "otto says:" interpretive line.
Customize
Where Otto becomes yours. Voice training (4 sliders + voice preview, trained on user's real posts — PRO feature). Per-platform tone shifters. Personality (name, accent color, persona, check-in frequency). Brand guardrails (never-use words, off-limits topics, content rule toggles). Posting schedule (24hr range slider + active days + max posts per day). Otto's leash (3-step autonomy: Ask first / Ask sometimes / Full auto).
Platforms
Honest connection manager. Each connected platform gets a full card with capability badge ("limited" / "business · full data" / "basic data"), a checked/X list of what Otto can actually do, and Otto's note interpreting what to do about it. Coming-soon section with vote-to-prioritize for Medium, Threads, Bluesky, Substack, Reddit. Privacy footer with one-click "Disconnect everything" — trust signal as a feature.
05 — Onboarding
6 steps · ~3 minutes · ends with an aha moment
  • Step 1 — Hello. Big Otto fills the screen. Italic intro: "think of me as your marketing intern." Two CTAs: Let's go / Maybe later.
  • Step 2 — Name me. Default is "otto" with suggestion chips (pico, mox, echo, nimo, ember). 5-second personalization moment that creates ownership.
  • Step 3 — Connect a platform. Pick one. Tiles have helper notes ("most students start here," "best analytics"). Skip option preserved.
  • Step 4 — Teach me your voice. Three sample slots, two pre-filled from the user's actual Vibe posts (zero friction since Vibe owns the data). Otto: "two is enough to start. i'll keep learning every time you post."
  • Step 5 — Set my leash. Three radio cards: Ask first (recommended) / Ask sometimes / Full auto (for the brave).
  • Step 6 — The aha moment. Otto generates a real LinkedIn draft from the user's voice samples. Otto's notes underneath call out what it observed ("lowercase opening · short sentences · meta-observation close"). CTA: "i'm ready, take me to my agent →"
Why this works
Steps 1-3 are emotional/personal (no data input), so user investment grows before any work is asked. Step 4 leverages Vibe's data advantage — we already have the user's posts, so we can pre-fill samples. Step 6 is the marketable moment users will tell friends about: "their AI made a post that actually sounded like me."
06 — Post-publish flow
5 frames · ~12 seconds · zero nag
  • t+0s — Post lands. Coral border + glow + "POSTED" badge on the new post. Otto stays quiet — user gets to enjoy the win first.
  • t+1.5s — Otto asks. Small dark bubble slides up from the corner ring with a pointer arrow. "want me to send this to your other 3 platforms?" Two buttons: Yes draft them / Skip. Platform mini-icons preview what Otto would do.
  • t+3s — Thinking state. Panel slides in (280px). Otto's thinking animation. Per-platform progress (LinkedIn ✓, Instagram drafting, X queued).
  • t+6s — Drafts ready. Full panel (320px). Briefing line at top. Three drafts with the recommended one highlighted as "PICK." X dimmed with "i'd skip" suggestion. Approve all 2 → CTA at bottom.
  • t+12s — Confirmation. Panel collapses. Original post now shows "otto · 2 platforms" badge. Small toast: "shipped to LinkedIn & Instagram. I'll watch how they do."

Paywall philosophy
Most metrics that compete with LinkedIn Premium go into Pro. The killer free-tier feature is Vibe Recruiter Intel itself (not LinkedIn views) — first-party data that costs LinkedIn nothing for us to expose. The Pro paywall is justified by voice training (real LLM cost), full insights, and full autonomy. Paywalls always delivered in Otto's voice, never as corporate CTAs ("i know who they are. wanna meet them?" instead of "Upgrade to see who viewed your profile").
09 — Brand principles for Otto
  • Otto has opinions, not just data. Every analytics surface includes Otto interpreting what the numbers mean and what to do about them.
  • Honest about gaps. When an API doesn't expose data, Otto says so. Transparency becomes the brand.
  • Respects "no." Otto never re-asks after a Skip. Never sends nudge notifications. Never escalates.
  • Earns trust gradually. Default autonomy is "Ask first." Users graduate to "Full auto" over weeks as Otto proves itself.
  • Lives in italic Fraunces. Otto's voice is always italicized + serif in the UI — visually distinct from system text.
  • Coral is action, purple is intelligence. Two-color accent system reinforces "Otto is alive and thinking."
  • Never "Sure!" or "Happy to help!" No AI-assistant boilerplate. Otto sounds like a person.

Strategic Direction

How Vibe Wins

Vibe's strategy has three audiences, three problems, and one defensible moat. The audiences are students, recruiters, and eventually universities. The problems are different for each. The moat — the thing that makes Vibe hard to copy — is Otto.

"Don't make Vibe more professional. Make it unmistakably useful to professionals."

For recruiters
The buyer is older. The product is for someone younger.
The most important tension in Vibe's design: the app is built for candidates (Gen Z and Millennials), but recruiters and hiring managers are often older and tend to skew toward formality. Solving for both is the design challenge — not picking one.
Recruiter value proposition:
  • Vibes let recruiters assess communication style, energy, and clarity of thought before any interview is scheduled.
  • Reactions show what the community values in a candidate — not raw follower counts or pay-to-play visibility.
  • Recruiter Mode gives a clean professional snapshot without forcing the recruiter to navigate a personality-first interface.
  • Zero spam model — candidates control who can reach them via the message request queue, so recruiter outreach is higher quality on both ends.
  • Otto Recruit (planned tier) — recruiters get their own Otto for outreach drafting, applicant classification, and interview coordination.
Language shift
Words matter. These are the ones we use.
Casual phrasing reads as "fun, not for work." Formal phrasing reads as "LinkedIn, but worse." The middle path — warm professional — has its own vocabulary.

For students
Five tools become one
Students currently juggle LinkedIn for the professional layer, Handshake for jobs, Canvas for academics, GroupMe or Discord for org chat, and Instagram for everything else. None of these connect. Each one is a checkbox, not a real tool. Vibe consolidates the professional + campus + social-identity layers into a single verified profile that follows the user from undergrad into early career.
The wedge: the Campus tab. Once a university adopts Vibe officially (career services, an entrepreneurship program, a department), every student in that program becomes a verified user with a built-in network on day one. Otto then handles the cross-post problem so they're not also juggling LinkedIn and Instagram and TikTok separately.
The moat — Otto
What LinkedIn cannot copy
A feed, comments, DMs, a profile page — LinkedIn already has all of these. They could rebuild Vibe's UI in a quarter if they wanted to. They cannot easily build Otto. Otto requires:
  • An LLM trained per-user on that user's voice. LinkedIn would have to either pay this LLM cost for half a billion users or charge for it (which would surface their pricing power problem).
  • Cross-platform OAuth integrations. LinkedIn has no incentive to help users post to Instagram or X — those are competitors.
  • First-party recruiter intel. Otto's killer feature on Vibe is showing the user who looked at their profile, for how long, and what they watched. LinkedIn could build this but would have to dismantle their Premium paywall to do it.
  • A character, not a feature. Otto has a voice, opinions, autonomy controls, a personality. LinkedIn's cultural identity will not let them ship something this distinctive.
For the full Otto spec — voice rules, API constraints, monetization scaffold, build scope — see the Otto — Flagship section.

Design Language

Visual & UX Direction

Vibe's visual identity is doing strategic work. Every color, font weight, and animation timing is calibrated so a recruiter lands on a profile and immediately reads "this is a serious, well-made product" before they read any of the personality content. Personality lives in the words, the photos, and Otto. The chrome stays calm.
The core principle
When a 45-year-old hiring manager lands on a Vibe profile, the design has to read "serious tool" within half a second. Personality should come through the content and copy — never through the UI being visually chaotic. This is the rule everything else follows from.
Typography
Fraunces + DM Sans
Fraunces (serif, display) paired with DM Sans (geometric sans). The pairing reads "editorial intelligence" — quality magazine meets modern startup — without going informal. Fraunces uses weights 400 / 700 / 900. DM Sans uses 300 through 700.
Hierarchy:
  • Page titles and section headers — Fraunces 900, letter-spacing -1px to -2px depending on size. Italic for emphasis (em tags) instead of bold.
  • Card titles and component headers — Fraunces 700, smaller letter-spacing.
  • Body copy — DM Sans 400 at 13-15px, line-height 1.6-1.7.
  • Labels and eyebrows — DM Sans 500-700 at 10-11px, letter-spacing 1.5px, uppercase.
  • Otto's voice — italic Fraunces, always. Visually distinct from system text and from the user's own writing.

Mint · #2ECC71Success state — confirmations, positive deltas, "saved" indicators.
The dual-accent rule: coral signals action — the user can do something, or has done something. Purple signals intelligence — Otto is thinking, drafting, or surfacing something. The two appear together on Otto's surfaces. They never appear together as a gradient outside Otto contexts.
Iconography
No emoji in the chrome — ever
Custom SVG icons throughout the UI. The no-emoji policy is non-negotiable for the visual chrome — buttons, nav, badges, callouts, section headers. The justification is strategic, not aesthetic: emoji read as "consumer app" and undercut the recruiter-facing serious tool we need Vibe to feel like.
The exception: emoji are permitted inside user-generated content — post bodies, comments, message bubbles, profile bios. That's the user being themselves, not the product chrome.
Interaction design
Snappy, crafted, never bouncy
  • Custom cursor — orange dot (instant, zero lerp) + trailing ring (lerp 0.28). Real Mac cursor hidden via cursor:none on every element. Cursor must use event delegation for hover state — static querySelectorAll patterns freeze on dynamic content.
  • Avatar hover — scale 1.06 even outside edit mode. Reinforces personality-first brand at the smallest scale.
  • Card lift — posts translate up 2px with deeper shadow on hover. Subtle.
  • Sticky subnav — IntersectionObserver scroll-spy highlights the active section automatically. Used on the profile page.
  • Reaction burst — springy scale animation on click, per-reaction glow color matching the icon family.
  • Edit mode — inline contenteditable + floating toolbar (B / I / U / Clear). Auto-save toast appears 1.8s after the last keystroke.
  • Modals slide up from the bottom — comments, event creation, opportunity detail. 280ms ease-out.
Otto motion language
Personality lives in animation
Otto has no face and no body. Its entire personality is conveyed through four animation states. The motion design has to do everything that a face would do.
  • Idle — slow breathing rings (2.8s ease-out infinite), one orbital node spinning (8s linear). Calm. The default 95% of the time.
  • Thinking — two counter-rotating orbits, multiple nodes, faster core pulse (1.6s). Visibly busy. Used during draft generation.
  • Briefing — vertical sound bars in alternating coral and purple, EQ-style. Otto is "speaking" with a long-form update.
  • Alert — bright pulse with a count chip. Something needs review. Never blinks, never escalates.
The core ratios: orbits at 8s linear (slow), 4.5s linear (medium), 3.2s reverse (fast). Breathing rings at 2.8-3.4s ease-out. Core pulse at 2.4s ease-in-out. These specific numbers matter — together they create Otto's "breathing" rhythm.

Indiana University — Director of Entrepreneurship

Meeting Questions

Prepared for a meeting with the Director of the Entrepreneurship Program at Indiana University. Goal: gather expert feedback on Vibe’s university strategy, validate the student-to-professional pipeline thesis, and understand what IU’s entrepreneurship community actually needs from a platform like this.

“No platform combines verified student identity + professional profile + campus job board + org discovery + live chat + calendar — all in one place.”

Section 1 — Validating the Problem
Is the gap we’re solving real?
  • When students are job-hunting or building their professional identity, what tools are they actually using — and where do those tools fall short?
  • LinkedIn is the default for professional networking. In your experience, do IU students actually use it effectively, or does it feel like a box they check?
  • What’s the biggest missed connection you see — students who could be finding opportunities, mentors, or collaborators but aren’t because the platform just isn’t built for them?
  • Is there a moment in a student’s IU experience where they feel most lost in terms of professional development? What triggers that?
  • How do students currently find out about campus career events, club callouts, and internship opportunities — and how fragmented is that process?
Section 2 — The Campus Feature
Is our IU hub the right answer?
  • Vibe has a Campus tab: a university-specific feed where students, clubs, and departments post announcements, events, opportunities, and callouts — all in one authenticated space. Does that solve a real pain point, or does something like that already exist?
  • From the entrepreneurship program’s perspective — how do you currently reach students when you have announcements, competitions, or mentorship opportunities? What’s the friction?
  • Would your program post on a platform like this? What would make you trust it enough to use it officially?
  • Handshake exists for career services. Canvas for academics. Discord and GroupMe for informal chat. Do students actually want one unified place, or is the fragmentation fine because each tool has a clear purpose?
  • If Vibe’s Campus tab existed right now — what’s the first thing you’d want to see posted on it from the entrepreneurship program?
Section 3 — Student Identity and Verification
How does being a verified student change things?
  • We’re planning a .edu email verification flow — sign up with personal email, verify student status with IU email. The badge unlocks the Campus tab and appears on profile. Does that feel right, or would students find the friction annoying?
  • How important is it to students that their professional profile is tied to a verified institution? Does “IU Verified” carry weight when talking to employers?
  • There’s a tension between a professional profile that follows you after graduation versus a campus identity tied to enrollment. How do you think about that transition?
  • Are there students who would specifically not want their professional network mixed with their campus social life?
Section 4 — Entrepreneurship and Startups
Does Vibe help student founders?
  • Student founders often need three things quickly: co-founders, early users, and feedback. How do IU student founders find those three things today — and where do they fail?
  • Vibe has a “find a co-founder” style post on the Campus feed — post what you’re building, what role you need, and people connect directly. Does that have real utility?
  • The Venture Club pitch competition is seeded as an example in our prototype. Events like that — is there a platform problem? Do students miss out because they don’t hear in time?
  • We have a short-form video feature called Vibes — 60 seconds, professional context. “Here’s the problem I’m solving and why it matters.” Would that resonate with student founders and investors?
  • If a student wanted to show a potential co-founder or investor that they’re serious — not just with a pitch deck but with how they think and communicate — what would that look like to you?
Section 5 — Go-to-Market and Growth
How do we actually get traction at IU?
  • If we launched Vibe at IU specifically — targeting entrepreneurship and Luddy School communities first — what would be the most credible way to introduce it? What makes students actually sign up?
  • Is there a class, club, or event where Vibe could naturally slot in as a tool — rather than asking students to adopt it cold?
  • What would it take for IU’s career services or entrepreneurship office to officially endorse or promote a platform like this?
  • Are there student organizations at IU currently underserved by existing tools — clubs that struggle to recruit members, communicate, or get event visibility?
  • We’ve heard from one student (Purdue, cybersecurity) that LinkedIn’s algorithm is broken — too much noise, not enough signal from people she actually interacts with. Is that a common complaint from IU students?
Section 6 — Big Picture and Pushback
What are we missing or getting wrong?
  • What’s your instinctive concern about a platform like Vibe? What would have to be true for it to fail?
  • LinkedIn has tried to make its product more human and less corporate for years and hasn’t succeeded. What makes you believe — or not — that a new platform can crack that?
  • Is there a student community at IU that would be the perfect early adopter — and one that definitely wouldn’t touch it?
  • If you were advising us as a founding team — what’s the one thing to get right in year one that most startups in this space get wrong?
  • We’re positioning this as “LinkedIn for people who exist outside of their resume.” Does that framing resonate with how you think about student professional development?
Things to bring / show
The working prototype: feed-b.html, campus.html, profile, opportunities. Show the Campus tab live — IU branding, verified student badge, real company listings, chat channels, RSVP events. Show the calendar. Show how a post flows from the campus feed into a student’s professional profile. Make it feel real.
